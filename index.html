Klint & Kandinsky

Morris Aguilar

Hilma af Klint and Wassily Kandinsky each developed systematic visual grammars in the early twentieth century, encoding spiritual and perceptual ideas into relationships between shape, color, and space. Klint's canvases balance geometric and organic forms with deliberate negative space. Kandinsky argued in Point and Line to Plane (1926) that each geometric element carries intrinsic emotional weight.

Five color palettes were extracted directly from source paintings as HSB values. A probabilistic grid algorithm places shapes across the canvas with a 30% cell-skip probability that encodes Klint's characteristic negative space ratios. Six geometric primitives drawn from both painters' vocabularies are extruded into three dimensions and placed sequentially, with depth revealed during a timed camera rotation.

This piece is the first entry in the Computational Art History series, a body of work that translates each painter's spatial and color logic into a parametric, browser-based generative system. Each composition is reproducible by seed and shareable by URL.
